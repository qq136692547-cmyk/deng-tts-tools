The Full TikTok Shop Fee Stack in 2026

Before you can calculate profit, you need to know every fee TikTok Shop charges. Most sellers only know the headline 6% referral fee. There are actually six distinct cost layers, and ignoring any one of them will inflate your profit estimate by 10% or more.

FeeRateNotes
Referral fee 6% of sale price Flat for most US categories; already includes payment processing.
Transaction fee $0.30 per order Flat fee charged on every completed transaction, separate from the 6%.
FBT fulfillment $2.86–$3.58 per unit Mandatory Fulfilled by TikTok fee for pick, pack, and ship. Varies by weight and zone.
Creator commission 0–30% of sale price Only if running affiliate campaigns. Set by you, not TikTok. Typical: 5–20%.
Cross-border fee ~1% of sale price Applies only to non-US sellers shipping into the US market. Domestic sellers skip this.
Refund administration fee 20% of original referral fee Charged when a buyer returns an item. Capped at $5 per SKU. You also lose the product if it's unsellable.

Before creator commission, the platform take on a $29.99 order is roughly $5.10–$5.82 (6% referral + $0.30 transaction + FBT). That's already 17–19% of revenue — not the 6% most sellers assume. Add a 10% creator commission and you're at $8.10 per unit before you've paid for the product itself.

These rates are current as of mid-2026 (source: FastMoss, Jun 2026).

Step-by-Step Profit Calculation

Let's work through a real example: a $29.99 beauty product with $9.50 COGS (manufacturing + inbound shipping to TikTok's warehouse), a 10% creator commission, and standard FBT fulfillment at $3.20 for a single-item order.

Step 1: Start with the selling price and subtract each fee layer.

Referral fee (6%):         -$1.80    ($29.99 × 0.06)
Transaction fee:           -$0.30    (flat per order)
FBT fulfillment:           -$3.20    (single-item, zone 1)
Creator commission (10%):  -$3.00    ($29.99 × 0.10)
                           ────────
Total fees:                -$8.30

COGS + inbound shipping:   -$9.50
                           ────────
NET PROFIT:                $12.19 per unit
NET MARGIN:                40.6%

Step 2: Check the margin. A 40.6% net margin is healthy for beauty — the category typically sees 25–40% margins (source: Dashboardly, Mar 2026). Below 20%, you need to raise price, cut COGS, or lower commission.

Step 3: Factor in ad spend. If you're running TikTok Shop Ads at 20% of revenue, that's another $6.00 per unit. Your profit drops to $6.19 — still positive, but the margin compresses to 20.6%. This is why tracking ad cost per unit, not just monthly totals, is critical.

If you'd only used the 6% referral fee number, you'd have estimated $18.69 per unit in profit. The real number with all fees and ad spend is $6.19 — a 67% overestimate that could sink your business in three months.

How Returns Eat Into Profit

Returns are the most underestimated cost on TikTok Shop. When a buyer returns an item, TikTok charges a refund administration fee equal to 20% of the original referral fee, and you may lose the product if it comes back unsellable.

For our $29.99 product, the refund admin fee is $0.36 per return. But the real cost is the lost product ($9.50 COGS) plus FBT already paid ($3.20) — $13.06 per returned unit if unsellable.

TikTok Shop return rates by category: Apparel 15–25%, Home goods 8–12%, Beauty 5–10% (source: Darkroom Agency, Jun 2026). Here's how different return rates impact your effective profit per unit sold:

Margin vs ROI: Which Number Matters?

Sellers often confuse net margin and ROI (Return on Investment). They measure different things, and tracking the wrong one can lead to bad pricing decisions.

Net margin = (Net Profit ÷ Selling Price) × 100. This tells you what percentage of revenue you keep. It's the number that matters for understanding whether your business is sustainable at scale. Banks and investors look at margin.

ROI = (Net Profit ÷ Total Cost) × 100. This tells you how efficiently you're turning invested capital into profit. It's the number that matters when deciding whether to reinvest in more inventory or switch products.

For our $29.99 product: net profit is $12.19, total cost (fees + COGS) is $17.80.

Net margin:  $12.19 ÷ $29.99 = 40.6%
ROI:         $12.19 ÷ $17.80 = 68.5%

A 40.6% margin is strong. A 68.5% ROI is also strong. But if you lower the price to $24.99 to boost sales volume, the margin might drop to 32% while ROI drops to 52% — both still positive, but you've given up $2.63 per unit. Use margin to decide if your price is sustainable; use ROI to decide if your product is worth reinvesting in.

Common Calculation Mistakes

These four mistakes account for nearly all "I thought I was profitable" scenarios:

1. Forgetting the $0.30 transaction fee. On a $9.99 product it's 3% of revenue — half the referral fee. On 1,000 orders/month, that's $300 unaccounted for.

2. Ignoring return costs entirely. Many sellers calculate profit assuming zero returns, then get hit with refund admin fees and lost inventory at month-end. Even a 5% return rate on our $29.99 example costs $0.65 per unit sold — money that comes directly out of your margin.

3. Calculating creator commission after tax. Creator commission is calculated on the full sale price, not on your post-fee revenue. A 10% commission on a $29.99 product is $3.00 — not $2.18 (which would be 10% of the $21.69 left after referral + transaction + FBT). This mistake understates commission cost by 27%.

4. Forgetting inbound shipping. Shipping from supplier to TikTok's FBT warehouse is part of COGS. If you pay $1.50 per unit inbound, that needs to be in your per-unit calculation — not tracked as a monthly logistics cost.
